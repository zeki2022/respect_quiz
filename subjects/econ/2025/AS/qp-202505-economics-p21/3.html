Full Model Answer (20/20 Marks)

Part (a) [8 Marks]

A subsidy is a payment by the government to producers to reduce costs and encourage output.

Diagram Analysis:
As shown in the diagram (see "Exam Mode" above), a subsidy reduces production costs, causing the Supply curve to shift to the right (from S to S1). This creates a surplus at the original price, forcing the market equilibrium to change. Consequently, the market price falls from P to P1, and the quantity traded increases from Q to Q1.

Evaluation (Expenditure):
The cost to the government depends on the Price Elasticity of Demand (PED). If demand is Price Inelastic (PED < 1), consumers are unresponsive to price drops, so Q rises only slightly, leading to smaller government expenditure. However, if demand is Price Elastic, Q rises significantly, causing a much larger expenditure.

Part (b) [12 Marks]

A tax on demerit goods aims to raise prices and discourage consumption to fix market failure.

Advantages: An increase in indirect tax shifts supply to the left, raising price. According to the law of demand, this should reduce the quantity consumed, thereby reducing negative externalities.

Limitations (Why not always best): However, demerit goods like tobacco are often addictive, meaning demand is Price Inelastic. A tax will significantly raise price but result in only a small reduction in consumption. Furthermore, high taxes may encourage black markets, bypassing quality controls.

Conclusion: Therefore, tax is not always the best way on its own. It is most effective when used in a multi-policy approach alongside information campaigns and regulation to address the root causes of consumption.

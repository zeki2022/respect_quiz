Full Answer

1. Diagram Analysis (AO1/AO2)

  • Shift: Subsidy reduces costs $\rightarrow$ Supply shifts Right ($S \rightarrow S_1$).
  • Equilibrium: Price falls ($P \rightarrow P_1$), Quantity rises ($Q \rightarrow Q_1$).
  • Vertical Distance: Represents the subsidy per unit.

2. Evaluation: Impact of PED (AO3)

The cost to government = $Subsidy \times Quantity (Q_1)$.

Inelastic Demand: $Q$ rises slightly. Price drops significantly. Gov Spending is smaller.

Elastic Demand: $Q$ rises significantly. Price drops slightly. Gov Spending is larger.

1. Arguments FOR Tax

• Increases price and reduces quantity consumed.

• Internalizes the negative externality (makes polluter pay).

• Generates government revenue.

2. Limitations (Why not "Always Best")

• Inelastic Demand: For addictive goods (cigarettes), price rise causes little drop in $Q$. Tax is ineffective at reducing consumption.

• Black Markets: High tax may lead to smuggling or illegal production.

• Regressive: Burdens low-income groups more.

3. Conclusion

Tax is effective but not "always the best" on its own. A multi-policy approach (e.g., Education + Regulation) is often better to address root causes.

(a) Analysis: A subsidy is a payment to producers that lowers marginal costs, shifting the supply curve right ($S$ to $S_1$). This creates excess supply at the original price, forcing price down to $P_1$ and quantity up to $Q_1$. Consumers benefit from lower prices, producers from higher revenues.

Evaluation: Government expenditure ($Subsidy \times Q_1$) depends on PED. If demand is price elastic, the % rise in Q is large, causing high expenditure. If inelastic, Q rises little, limiting costs.

(b) Assessment: Indirect taxes raise the price of demerit goods, which should reduce consumption according to the law of demand. This helps internalize negative externalities.

However, demand for demerit goods is often price inelastic due to addiction (e.g., tobacco). A tax will raise price but reduce quantity very little, failing its primary objective. Additionally, high taxes may encourage black markets.

Conclusion: Therefore, tax is not always the best single solution. It is most effective when combined with education (to shift demand left) and regulation.

1 Can South Africa escape from its economic difficulties?

South Africa has serious economic difficulties. Despite its vast natural resources, the main economic indicators show a country in trouble. As Fig. 1.1 shows, despite a fall over the last year, unemployment remains very high. It is amongst the highest in the world, particularly for young people, and this has led to one of the highest and most persistent levels of income inequality in the world, with a Gini coefficient of 0.67.

Economic growth is amongst the worst in Africa and this, together with high government expenditure, means that the country has had a fiscal deficit of more than 4% of GDP in recent years. Despite falls in the value of South Africa's currency (rand), the current account of the balance of payments is projected to move to a significant deficit of 2.3% of GDP in 2023 and to deteriorate further to about 2.5% of GDP in 2024. South Africa is also suffering extended power cuts of up to 10 hours per day due to the collapse of the state electricity provider. What can be done?

In a March 2023 statement, the International Monetary Fund (IMF) recommended that the South African government should adopt the following policies:

  • Improvements in infrastructure, particularly electricity supplies and transport links. This should be achieved by promoting more private sector investment.
  • Encourage more competition within South Africa and free trade with neighbouring countries by reducing protection. Closer links with the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) should be established.
  • To tackle the very high rates of unemployment and income inequality, a range of policies has been suggested. These include setting the minimum national wage at an appropriate rate to encourage more people back into the workforce. This should be done alongside policies to strengthen employment protection. In addition, other policies include improving levels of education and training and better support for the transition from school to work. Finally, policies to promote entrepreneurship need to be introduced.

However, will these policies help South Africa escape from its economic difficulties?

2 Minimum wage: Directly raises incomes for low-wage workers, reducing wage inequality.
3 Education and training: Increases human capital, enabling workers to access higher-skilled, better-paying jobs.

Part (c) - Critical Evaluation

1 Price Elasticity of Demand (PED): The effectiveness of depreciation depends on whether the Marshall-Lerner condition is satisfied: $$PED_X + PED_M > 1$$ where $PED_X$ is PED for exports and $PED_M$ is PED for imports.
2 J-Curve Effect: In the short run, depreciation may worsen the current account as quantities adjust slowly (inelastic demand). Only in the long run does improvement occur as consumers find substitutes.
3 Inflationary pressure: Depreciation increases import prices, which can cause cost-push inflation, potentially offsetting any competitiveness gains.
4 Conclusion: Continued falls in the rand may not reduce the current account deficit in the short term, and effectiveness depends on elasticities and time horizon.

Part (b) [2 Marks]

Impact on firms: A high unemployment rate means firms face a larger pool of available labor, which may allow them to recruit workers at lower wage rates due to reduced bargaining power of workers. Alternatively, high unemployment reduces aggregate demand in the economy, leading to weaker demand for firms' products and potentially lower profits.

Impact on government: High unemployment reduces tax revenue from income taxes as fewer people are earning taxable income. Simultaneously, the government faces increased expenditure on unemployment benefits and other social welfare programs, exacerbating the fiscal deficit.

Part (c) [4 Marks]

Continued falls in the value of the South African rand may lead to a reduction in the current account deficit, but this outcome is not guaranteed and depends on several factors.

Theoretical mechanism: Depreciation makes South African exports cheaper in foreign markets and imports more expensive domestically. This should increase the quantity demanded of exports and decrease the quantity demanded of imports, improving net exports $(X - M)$ and reducing the current account deficit.

Critical evaluation: The effectiveness depends crucially on the price elasticity of demand (PED) for exports and imports. According to the Marshall-Lerner condition, depreciation improves the current account only if $PED_X + PED_M > 1$. In the short run, demand tends to be inelastic as consumers and producers need time to adjust, potentially causing a J-curve effect where the deficit worsens before improving.

Additional considerations: Depreciation may also fuel inflation through higher import prices, eroding competitiveness gains. Furthermore, South Africa's export structure (heavy reliance on commodities with relatively inelastic demand) may limit the benefits of depreciation.

Conclusion: While continued rand depreciation could reduce the current account deficit in the long run if elasticities are favorable, short-term effects may be adverse, and complementary policies to enhance export competitiveness are likely necessary.

Part (d) [6 Marks]

Closer membership of the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) may help South Africa achieve growth, but the extent of this help is contingent on several factors.

Potential benefits (analysis): AfCFTA provides access to a market of over 1.3 billion people, enabling export-led growth through increased foreign demand. This stimulates aggregate demand (AD) and can create economies of scale for South African firms. Additionally, increased competition may force domestic firms to become more efficient, potentially increasing aggregate supply (AS) through productivity gains.

Limitations and drawbacks (evaluation): South African industries may face intense competition from more efficient producers in other African countries, potentially leading to deindustrialization in vulnerable sectors. The country's infrastructure constraints, particularly electricity shortages, severely limit its ability to expand production for export markets. Benefits may be unevenly distributed, with skilled workers and capital owners gaining disproportionately, potentially exacerbating inequality.

Time dimension: Short-term adjustment costs (job losses, firm closures) may outweigh benefits, while long-term gains depend on successful structural transformation.

Conclusion: AfCFTA membership can contribute to growth but is not a panacea. Its effectiveness depends on complementary domestic reforms to address infrastructure deficits, improve education and skills, and support vulnerable sectors through the transition.

Part (e) [6 Marks]

The suggested supply-side policies are likely to reduce income inequality in South Africa, but their effectiveness is subject to significant qualifications.

How policies may reduce inequality (analysis): Infrastructure improvements, particularly in transport, increase labor mobility, allowing workers to access higher-paying jobs. A minimum wage directly raises incomes for low-wage workers, compressing the wage distribution. Education and training enhance human capital, enabling upward mobility and access to higher-skilled, better-remunerated employment. Entrepreneurship promotion can create new income opportunities.

Limitations and challenges (evaluation): South Africa's fiscal constraints (deficit exceeding 4% of GDP) limit the government's capacity to finance infrastructure and social programs. The minimum wage may increase unemployment if set above market equilibrium, potentially worsening poverty. Education reforms take decades to yield benefits, while inequality requires more immediate attention. Deep-seated structural barriers (apartheid legacy, spatial segregation, skills mismatches) may persist despite these policies.

Implementation considerations: The sequencing and targeting of policies matter. For instance, infrastructure investment in marginalized regions combined with localized education programs may be more effective than uniform national policies.

Conclusion: While the recommended policies address key drivers of inequality and are likely to reduce income disparity over time, their success depends on careful design, adequate financing, and addressing the structural roots of South Africa's exceptionally high inequality (Gini coefficient of 0.67).
